Section 1: Overview

Originator:Minnesota Department of Natural Resources (DNR)

Title: Minnesota Wild and Scenic River Districts

Abstract: District boundaries for wild, scenic, and recreational rivers designated under the Minnesota State Wild and Scenic Rivers Act. Includes portions of the Minnesota River, Mississippi River, Rum River, Kettle River, North Fork of the Crow River and the Cannon River.

Purpose: To easily identify district boundaries on maps or using GIS software.

Time Period of Content Date: October 19, 2006

Currentness Reference: October 19, 2006
Some district boundaries were determined by using distance buffers from a river shape file that was made from DNR 24k Lakes data. The DNR 24k Lakes Data are derived primarily from the National Wetlands Inventory (NWI) which are based on 1990 vintage aerial photography. Some 200 quads of data are based on USGS quadrangle maps, which vary widely in vintage from the early 1980s to early 1990s.

Progress: Complete

Maintenance and Update Frequency: As needed

Spatial Extent of Data: Portions of the Minnesota River (Stearns Co., Wright Co., Sherburne Co., Hennepin Co., and Anoka Co.), Mississippi River (Lac Qui Parle Co., Chippewa Co., Yellow Medicine Co., Renville Co., and Redwood Co.), Rum River (Mille Lacs Co., Sherburne Co., Isanti Co., and Anoka Co.), Kettle River (Pine Co.), North Fork of the Crow River (Meeker Co.) and the Cannon River (Rice Co., Dakota Co., and Goodhue Co.).

Access Constraints: None

Use Constraints: District shape files are approximate in many areas. The actual district boundaries are described in Minnesota Rules 6105. The only definitive way to determine if a subject property is within a district is to have the property surveyed and compare that information to the district descriptions in the rules.

Section 2: Data Quality

Attribute Accuracy:

Logical Consistency: Not specifically assessed

Completeness: In some areas the district boundary is described as a set distance from the ordinary high water mark. No geographic data was available for the ordinary high water mark. DNR 24k Lakes data was used to approximate the ordinary high water mark. In cases where there are step riverbanks this data may be very accurate. In cases where there are shallow or gradual riverbanks the ordinary high water mark, and thus the district boundary is more inland than represented in the data.

In the case of the North Fork of the Crow River, no DNR 24k Lakes data was available to identify the edge of the river. In this case a line representing the center of the river was used as the starting point for the district measurement. The actual district boundary will be further inland than is represented as the ordinary high water mark is on the edge of the river rather than the center.
